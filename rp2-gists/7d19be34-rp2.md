**Score: 133/140**

## Overall
This is a strong RP-2. The proposal now has a clear center: behavior-guidance systems simplify complex behavior through generalized instructions, and that simplification can hide assumptions that fail across users and contexts. That is a solid requirements engineering problem, and the dog-training case works well as a concrete example without taking over the entire project.

The biggest improvement here is the framing. This draft no longer reads like “a paper about dog training.” It reads like a paper about assumption-driven risks in guidance systems, with dog training as the case study. That is a much better fit for the course and a much stronger research direction.

## What is working well

### 1. The systems framing is much better
This is the strongest feature of the draft. You have shifted the center of gravity to behavior-guidance systems as a broader class of software, which makes the proposal more generalizable and much more clearly tied to requirements engineering.

### 2. The problem statement is well developed
You explain the tradeoff clearly: guidance systems simplify behavior in order to improve usability, but that simplification often depends on assumptions that may not hold in real settings. The proposal also does a good job identifying why that matters:
- users may treat the guidance as authoritative
- context varies more than the system may acknowledge
- hidden assumptions can reduce reliability and trust

That is a good, substantial problem statement.

### 3. The research questions are focused and usable
The questions are much more deliberate now. They build well from one another and stay aligned with the central problem. In particular, the question about balancing usability/simplicity against adaptability/contextual accuracy is strong because it gets at a real design tradeoff.

### 4. The proposed approach is credible
The three-phase structure is clear and believable:
- identify assumptions
- examine failure scenarios
- develop an evaluation framework

That is a sensible progression, and it gives the proposal a useful internal structure.

### 5. The response to feedback is effective
You clearly show how the project changed in response to the earlier concern. The revision is not cosmetic; it reflects a real conceptual tightening.

## What to strengthen

### 1. Be careful that the project does not become too broad again
Even though this version is better scoped, “behavior-guidance systems” is still a large category. The dog-training case helps, but the final proposal should continue to make clear what the primary evidence base actually is. Otherwise the project could drift into a broad discussion of many kinds of instructional systems without enough depth.

### 2. The related-work section could say a bit more about software systems beyond the dog-training case
The current related work is solid, but it leans heavily on the case domain. That is understandable, but the next version would be stronger if it also included just a bit more from requirements/context-aware/adaptive systems literature to keep the software-engineering side equally visible.

### 3. Clarify what the resulting framework will look like
The proposal says it will produce a requirements-oriented evaluation framework, which is promising. The next version should say a little more about whether that framework is intended to be:
- a checklist
- a categorization model
- a set of evaluative dimensions
- a way of writing/validating requirements

You do not need the final form yet, but a slightly clearer picture of the output would help.

## Suggestions for the next iteration

### 1. Keep the dog-training case central but clearly bounded
That case is doing useful work here. Keep it, but make sure it remains the primary example rather than one example among many.

### 2. Make the intended contribution more explicit
A likely contribution is something like: a structured way to identify and evaluate simplifying assumptions in behavior-guidance systems before they produce unreliable guidance across contexts.

### 3. Strengthen the bridge between assumptions and requirements decisions
The final version should make especially clear how hidden assumptions affect requirement specification, validation, and reliability claims.

## Bottom line
This is one of the stronger drafts in the batch. It is clearly improved, conceptually better centered, and much more aligned with software requirements engineering. The next step is mainly disciplined refinement: keep the scope under control, slightly strengthen the broader RE grounding, and clarify the shape of the resulting framework.
