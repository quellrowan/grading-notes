## Overall
This is a meaningful RP-2 with a clear central problem: safety and security requirements in DOD UAV autonomous navigation can conflict, and existing standards and integrated methods do not clearly tell engineers how to prioritize when those conflicts cannot be eliminated. That is a legitimate requirements engineering issue, and the proposal is notably better once it centers prioritization rather than just conflict detection.

The draft also shows good improvement in structure. It is much clearer now what the paper is trying to answer and how the supporting questions relate to the main one.

## What is working well

### 1. The problem is more clearly centered on prioritization
This is the biggest improvement. The proposal now makes it clear that the research problem is not merely “safety and security interact,” but rather “how should engineers prioritize when requirements collide?” That gives the paper a real center.

### 2. The research questions have a sensible structure
RQ1 is clearly the main question, and RQ2/RQ3 now do supporting work. That is a good improvement and makes the project easier to follow.

### 3. The approach is feasible within the course constraints
Using public standards, literature, and incident/vulnerability reporting is a sensible way to keep the project realistic. The three-phase structure is understandable and reasonably matched to the questions.

### 4. The response to feedback is direct
You clearly show what the earlier concerns were and how you responded to them. That revision work is visible and helpful.

## What to strengthen

### 1. The writing still needs another round of tightening
The draft is improved, but some sentences remain awkward or grammatically rough, and a few ideas are repeated. This matters because the proposal is conceptually dense. Cleaner wording will make the argument feel more authoritative and easier to trust.

### 2. The proposed “heuristic” needs a clearer description
You say the project will develop a preliminary prioritization heuristic, which is promising, but the reader still does not know what that will look like. The next version should say more explicitly whether that heuristic is meant to be:
- a decision aid
- a categorization scheme
- a set of prioritization criteria
- a tradeoff framework

### 3. Be careful with the evidence available for RQ1
This is the hardest part of the project. Since you do not have interviews or internal program data, it may be difficult to say how engineers actually prioritize. The draft partly acknowledges this, which is good. I would continue to frame the result carefully as a proposed reasoning framework derived from public materials, not a verified account of current DOD practice.

### 4. Related work could do a bit more synthesis
The section identifies relevant sources, but it would help to say more directly what they do not yet provide. The gap seems to be that integrated methods surface conflicts but do not help resolve them. That is strong—state it bluntly.

## Suggestions for the next iteration

### 1. Tighten the prose line by line
This will help a lot.

### 2. Clarify the final artifact
A slightly clearer picture of the prioritization heuristic would strengthen the entire proposal.

### 3. Keep the claims disciplined
The project is worthwhile even if it offers a conceptual prioritization framework rather than a validated model of actual practice.

## Bottom line
This is a good and improving RP-2. The topic is now much better centered, the questions are more coherent, and the project has a plausible path forward. The next step is careful refinement: cleaner writing, clearer description of the resulting heuristic, and continued honesty about what the evidence can and cannot support.
