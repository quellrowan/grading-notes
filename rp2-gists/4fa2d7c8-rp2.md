## Overall
This is an excellent RP-2 and one of the strongest drafts in the set. The proposal is focused, well structured, and very clearly grounded in requirements engineering. You have a concrete case, a defined analytical lens, a plausible evidence base, and a thoughtful response to feedback. Just as importantly, the draft shows restraint: it makes an interesting claim without trying to solve everything.

The strongest feature here is the framing. You are not treating the bypasses themselves as the topic in isolation. You are treating them as evidence of upstream requirements failures, especially around predictable misuse, stakeholder conflict, and cross-feature interaction. That is exactly the right move.

## What is working well

### 1. The problem statement is sharp and compelling
The proposal explains clearly why these bypasses matter. The issue is not that clever users found one trick; it is that multiple foreseeable circumvention paths suggest design-level blind spots. That is a real requirements concern, and you explain it well.

### 2. The scope is disciplined
This draft does a very good job avoiding the “everything about parental control systems” trap. You stay with one system, a bounded set of representative bypasses, and a specific interpretive lens. That makes the project feel researchable and credible.

### 3. The research questions are strong
The questions are aligned and purposeful. In particular, RQ1 is nicely sharpened by the explicit foreseeability criteria. That is a real improvement because it gives the project a clearer evaluative standard instead of relying on intuition.

### 4. The proposed approach is clear and believable
The stepwise structure works well:
- establish the evidence base
- build a failure taxonomy
- apply counterfactual RE analysis
- generalize carefully

That sequence gives the paper a strong backbone.

### 5. The response to feedback is excellent
This is one of the best response-to-feedback sections in the batch. You explain not only what changed, but why those changes improved the project. That makes the revision process visible in a useful way.

## What to strengthen

### 1. Keep the generalization step modest
The project is strongest when it stays anchored in Screen Time and uses other domains as careful extensions rather than as co-equal cases. The transferable insights are promising, but do not let that section grow so much that it starts to compete with the main case.

### 2. Be careful not to overstate what the evidence can prove
You are already fairly disciplined here, but the next version should continue to distinguish between:
- demonstrating plausibility
- showing repeated patterns
- proving what Apple knew or when it knew it

The proposal does not need the strongest possible claim to be persuasive.

### 3. Consider tightening a few sections for concision
The draft is strong, but there are places where the same idea is restated in slightly different language. A little trimming in the final version will make the argument even sharper.

## Suggestions for the next iteration

### 1. Keep the bypass set intentional
Choose the representative bypasses that best illustrate distinct failure categories and build the paper around those.

### 2. Preserve the evidence/lens distinction
That distinction is one of the most conceptually successful things in the draft.

### 3. Make the likely contribution very explicit near the end of the introduction
You already imply it well; one crisp sentence will make it even stronger.

## Bottom line
This is a very strong RP-2. The topic is focused, the requirements lens is clear, the approach is plausible, and the revision work is obvious. The remaining job is mostly refinement and careful discipline, not major conceptual repair.
