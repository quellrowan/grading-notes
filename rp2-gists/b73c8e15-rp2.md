# RP-2 Feedback

**Score: 131/140**

## Overall
This is a solid RP-2 with a clearer center than many early-stage LLM proposals tend to have. The draft identifies a real requirements problem: teams still need to define acceptable behavior, risk boundaries, escalation expectations, and validation criteria even when the assistant itself is probabilistic and variable. That is a worthwhile direction, and the proposal is much stronger because it focuses on task-oriented assistants rather than “AI systems” in general.

The proposal is especially effective when it frames the issue as a requirements gap rather than a model-improvement problem. That keeps the project aligned with the course and gives it a realistic scope.

## What is working well

### 1. The problem statement is clear and relevant
You explain the practical issue well: assistants can be useful without being fully predictable, but product teams still need to define what acceptable behavior means. That is a real software/requirements engineering challenge.

### 2. The research questions are more focused now
The questions are clearly improved from a more exploratory starting point. They now ask about:
- requirement categories
- differences across risk levels
- validation and misuse scenarios

That is a manageable and coherent structure.

### 3. The related work supports the direction of the paper
The combination of adaptive systems, RE for AI, ML engineering, hallucination work, and NIST guidance gives the proposal a decent foundation. It shows that you are trying to situate the project between high-level AI risk guidance and actual system requirements.

### 4. The proposed approach is plausible for this assignment
A conceptual and comparative study is a good fit. Comparing lower-risk and higher-risk assistant contexts is a sensible way to keep the topic concrete while still saying something broader.

### 5. The response to feedback is honest and useful
You clearly identify the earlier problem—too broad, too exploratory—and show how you narrowed the scope in response.

## What to strengthen

### 1. The final output of the project needs to be named more explicitly
Right now the proposal suggests a framework of requirement categories, but the reader still has to infer the shape of that framework. The next version should say more clearly what the artifact is meant to be:
- a taxonomy?
- a requirements template?
- a risk-based prioritization framework?
- a set of reusable requirement patterns?

### 2. The use-context comparison needs a bit more specificity
The lower-risk/higher-risk comparison is promising, but it is still somewhat tentative. The next draft would be stronger if you choose the contexts more firmly and explain why those are the right comparison points.

### 3. Related work could be synthesized a little more sharply
The sources are relevant, but the section would be stronger if it ended with a more direct statement of the gap: what exactly is missing between existing guidance and the kind of requirements artifact you want to create?

### 4. Be careful that the project does not drift into broad AI governance language
The draft is strongest when it stays close to concrete software-assistant behavior. Keep it there. If it drifts too far into general trust/ethics/risk language, the requirements focus will weaken.

## Suggestions for the next iteration

### 1. Choose the comparison contexts firmly
A clear pair of contexts will help the whole proposal feel more anchored.

### 2. Make the contribution more explicit
You are likely building toward a small framework for specifying acceptable behavior in task-oriented LLM assistants under different risk conditions. Say that directly.

### 3. Tie validation more concretely to the requirements categories
The validation section becomes stronger if the reader can see exactly how each requirement category would be checked.

## Bottom line
This is a good RP-2. The topic is clearly more focused now, the research questions are more deliberate, and the proposal has a plausible path forward. The next step is mainly to sharpen the intended output and lock down the comparison design so the final proposal feels even more concrete.
