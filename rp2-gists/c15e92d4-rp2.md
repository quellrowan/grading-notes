## Overall
This is a promising RP-2 with a relevant topic and a clearer requirements-centered direction than the earlier version likely had. The proposal identifies an important issue: hallucinations in LLM-supported software engineering tools are often treated only as model limitations, when they may also reflect missing or under-specified system requirements around verification, uncertainty, and acceptable behavior.

That is a worthwhile angle. The proposal is strongest when it stays close to that idea and focuses on software engineering tools rather than drifting into general AI reliability language.

## What is working well

### 1. The problem framing is improved
The shift toward hallucination as a requirements problem is the right move. That gives the topic a real place in this course and helps distinguish it from a generic AI discussion.

### 2. The application domain is more concrete
Focusing on software engineering tasks helps a lot. It gives the proposal a clearer context and makes the consequences of hallucination easier to reason about.

### 3. The research questions are generally aligned
The questions point toward:
- how hallucinations show up in software tasks
- what requirements are missing
- what requirement patterns might reduce the risk
- where prioritization should differ by context

That is a reasonable structure.

### 4. The response to feedback shows useful narrowing
You clearly took the earlier advice to narrow the scope and make the requirements frame more explicit.

## What to strengthen

### 1. The related-work section needs stronger sources and a more academic tone
Right now the section leans heavily on industry or general-source framing, and it is thinner than it should be for this stage. Since RP-2 is supposed to show that the project is grounded in existing work, I would strengthen this section with more scholarly sources directly related to:
- RE for AI systems
- trust/verification requirements
- uncertainty communication
- LLM behavior in software engineering tasks

### 2. The proposed approach is still a bit list-like
The approach has the right pieces, but it currently reads more like an outline of intentions than a fully connected study design. The next version should explain the logic linking the steps together. For example: how does the hallucination taxonomy lead into the requirement patterns, and how will the scenarios be used to evaluate them?

### 3. The terminology should be tightened
Some terms are doing broad work without being fully defined, especially “hallucination,” “requirements,” and “mitigate.” The next version should be a little more precise about what kinds of hallucination matter in this project and what type of requirements artifact you are proposing.

### 4. The final contribution needs to be named more clearly
It sounds like the likely contribution is a small set of requirement patterns or categories for managing hallucination risk in software engineering tools. That is promising. I would begin stating that directly.

## Suggestions for the next iteration

### 1. Upgrade the related-work section first
That is the biggest weakness right now.

### 2. Turn the approach into a connected argument
Explain not just what you will do, but why those steps answer the questions.

### 3. Keep the focus on software engineering tools
That is what makes the proposal workable and relevant.

## Bottom line
This is a good direction and a worthwhile topic, but it still needs another round of development to feel as grounded and mature as the stronger drafts in the batch. The next version should especially strengthen the related work, tighten the terminology, and make the approach feel more analytically connected.
