## Overall
This is a strong RP-1. The proposal has a clear conceptual center, and the narrowing from RP-0 is visible and effective. In particular, the move to focus on **ambiguity in prompts** as a requirements problem gives the project a much stronger and more researchable shape. Treating prompts as lightweight requirements artifacts is an interesting and well-motivated idea, and the proposal does a good job explaining why that framing matters.

One of the strongest features of this draft is that it does not treat prompt failure only as a model issue. Instead, it asks whether at least some of that failure should be understood as a **specification problem**. That is a valuable software requirements engineering angle and makes this a good fit for the course.

## What is working well

### 1. The core idea is clear and interesting
The central claim—that prompts often function like lightweight requirements artifacts—is strong. You explain that clearly and tie it to familiar requirements concepts such as:
- ambiguity
- incompleteness
- inconsistent interpretation
- downstream failure caused by unclear specification

That makes the proposal feel grounded in course concepts rather than just adjacent to them.

### 2. The narrowing is effective
This is one of the better examples in the batch of meaningful revision. You clearly moved away from trying to cover:
- ambiguity
- validation
- misuse
- prioritization
- output drift
- general prompt quality

all at once. Focusing specifically on **ambiguity** and its relationship to **consistency of output** was the right move. The draft is much more disciplined because of that choice.

### 3. The problem statement is well aligned with the research direction
The statement does a good job explaining:
- why prompts matter in AI-supported software tasks
- why natural language makes them vulnerable to RE-style quality problems
- why unreliable outputs should not automatically be blamed only on the model
- why a requirements lens might offer a more disciplined way to understand the issue

That creates a coherent problem statement with a clear intellectual center.

### 4. The response to feedback is strong
You show good awareness of what changed and why. In particular, you clearly identify:
- that the earlier version had too many parallel ideas
- that the new version centers ambiguity
- that output consistency is now treated as the main observable consequence

That is exactly the kind of refinement RP-1 is supposed to demonstrate.

## What to strengthen

### 1. Some of the research questions are still doing a lot at once
The first two questions are strong and closely aligned with the new center. The third and fourth, however, start to broaden the project again a little. In particular:
- the question about which prompt features matter most
- and the question about whether failure is really a prompt/specification problem rather than a model-performance problem

both move toward larger comparative or explanatory claims.

Those are good questions, but together they may make the project harder to manage if the final proposal is only 3–5 pages. I would encourage you to keep the center on **ambiguity and consistency**, and make sure the later questions support that rather than opening additional subprojects.

### 2. Be careful with the “prompts are functionally very similar to requirements” claim
This is a strong and interesting claim, but it is also one that will need to be handled carefully. It works well as a motivating idea, but in the full proposal you may want to present it a little more cautiously. For example, rather than treating prompts and requirements as fully equivalent, you might frame prompts as **requirements-like artifacts** or as artifacts that share several quality concerns with traditional requirements.

That would preserve the strength of the idea while making the argument a little more precise.

### 3. Clarify what “consistency” means in the project
You use consistency in a sensible way, but the next version should make it slightly clearer what kind of consistency you mean. For example:
- consistency across repeated runs of the same prompt?
- consistency in adherence to constraints?
- consistency of output structure?
- consistency across models?

The proposal hints at several of these. The next step is to decide which of them matters most for the project.

## Suggestions for the next iteration

### 1. Keep ambiguity as the center
This is the strongest thing the proposal currently has. I would not widen it again. The best full proposal is likely one that stays firmly centered on:
- prompt ambiguity
- requirements-style structure
- consistency of interpretation/output in software-related tasks

That is already enough for a strong paper.

### 2. Consider tightening the research questions slightly
A good next step would be to make sure each question clearly supports the same central aim. Right now, the proposal is close, but it may benefit from slightly tightening the set so that:
- one question focuses on identifying ambiguity through an RE lens
- one focuses on consistency/adherence outcomes
- one focuses on which structural features seem to help reduce ambiguity

That would keep the project coherent and manageable.

### 3. Think ahead about the evidence source
For the next iteration, it would help to begin clarifying what evidence the project would rely on. For example:
- repeated prompt trials
- comparison of structured vs. informal prompts
- software-related prompt tasks with explicit constraints
- cross-model comparison only if it remains manageable

You do not need full methodology yet, but the project will be stronger if the next version begins to indicate what kind of evidence could support the claims.

## Bottom line
This is a thoughtful and much improved RP-1. The conceptual hook is strong, the narrowing is real, and the proposal now has a much clearer requirements engineering center. The next step is to keep that focus, clarify what kind of consistency the project cares most about, and make sure the research questions stay tightly aligned with the core issue of ambiguity in prompts.
