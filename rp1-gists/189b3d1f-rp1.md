## Overall
This is a strong RP-1. The proposal is focused, clear, and much better scoped than a broader project on “fun,” “engagement,” or subjective game experience in general would be. Narrowing the project to **telemetry-based proxies for challenge** gives you a much more workable and researchable problem. This is a good fit for the assignment because it keeps the emphasis on **problem clarity, research question quality, and refinement of scope**, rather than trying to turn the proposal into a full methods or design paper too early.

## What is working well

### 1. The problem statement is focused and coherent
The strongest feature of this draft is that it clearly defines the problem as a **validity problem**. You are not just saying that challenge is subjective; you are explaining why that matters in a requirements and evaluation context. The examples of:
- retry counts
- time-to-mastery
- completion rates
- hint usage

help make the issue concrete. You also explain well why these metrics are useful and why they are risky at the same time. That balance makes the problem statement feel thoughtful rather than simplistic.

### 2. You chose a manageable center
The narrowing from broad subjective game design ideas to a single concept—**challenge**—is a very good move. So is the decision to focus on telemetry proxies rather than trying to evaluate every possible way games create player experience. That gives the project a clearer center and makes the research questions more aligned.

This is exactly the kind of narrowing RP-1 is supposed to show.

### 3. The research questions are well aligned
The research questions are generally strong and follow a sensible progression:
- What counts as a proxy for challenge?
- What assumptions support those proxies?
- When do they become ambiguous or misleading?
- How might multiple signals improve reliability?

That sequence makes sense. The questions stay tied to the core problem rather than drifting into unrelated areas of game design.

### 4. The response to feedback is effective
You clearly explain what changed:
- the original topic was too broad
- the focus is now specifically on telemetry-based proxies
- the proposal now centers on one subjective concept rather than many

That makes the revision visible and meaningful. The RP-1 reads like an actual refinement rather than a small wording change.

## What to strengthen

### 1. Clarify what “validly represent” means
This phrase is central to the project, but right now it still carries some ambiguity. That is normal at this stage, but for the next version you will want to sharpen what kind of validity you mean. For example:
- correspondence to reported player experience?
- usefulness as a design signal?
- consistency across player types or contexts?
- interpretability for developers?

At the moment, the idea is strong, but the standard of validity is still somewhat implicit. The next version should start to make that more explicit.

### 2. Watch the scope of the final question
The final question about combining telemetry signals is interesting, but it risks pushing the project slightly toward solution design or evaluation-model design if it grows too much. Right now it is still acceptable, but in the full proposal you should be careful not to let the project split into two separate goals:
- analyzing whether current proxies are valid
- designing a better telemetry framework

Those are related, but the first is probably the stronger center for this project. Keep the core problem primary.

### 3. Ground the project in a likely evidence source
The proposal is conceptually strong, but for the next version it would help to begin thinking about what kind of evidence or examples the project would use. For example:
- prior studies on player telemetry
- case studies from specific game genres
- examples where behavioral metrics conflicted with reported player experience
- design literature on challenge tuning and balancing

You do not need a full methods section yet, but you do need a clearer sense of what kind of material would let you investigate the problem.

## Suggestions for the next iteration

### 1. Define the scope of “challenge” a little more carefully
Challenge can mean different things:
- skill difficulty
- pacing difficulty
- frustration threshold
- learning curve
- persistence required

You do not need to resolve all of that immediately, but it would help to identify what sense of challenge your project is most concerned with. That will make the final proposal more precise.

### 2. Consider whether you need a case context
You may eventually want to anchor the proposal in:
- a particular game genre
- a particular kind of telemetry environment
- or a specific design context

That is not required yet, but some grounding may help keep the project from staying too abstract in the next draft.

### 3. Keep the contribution analytical, not overly solution-oriented
The strongest version of this proposal is one that asks:
- when telemetry proxies work
- when they fail
- what assumptions they depend on
- and what kinds of ambiguity they create

That is already a valuable contribution. Be careful not to overcommit too early to building a new model or evaluation system unless that truly becomes necessary.

## Bottom line
This is a strong RP-1. The proposal is focused, the problem is clear, and the questions are aligned with the core issue. The next step is to make key terms like **challenge** and **validity** a little more precise and to identify what evidence base will support the full proposal.
