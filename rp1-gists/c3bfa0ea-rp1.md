**Score: 100/120**

## Overall
This is an interesting topic, and your enthusiasm for it definitely comes through. There is a real and worthwhile requirements engineering problem somewhere in this space: how developers specify and validate intended player experience in systems where success depends not only on correct functionality, but on sustained engagement, perceived challenge, and player motivation over time.

That said, this RP-1 still feels broader and less focused than it needs to be. Right now the draft reads partly like a topic exploration and partly like a proposal. The main issue is not that the topic is bad — it is that the proposal still has too many possible centers competing with one another. The next step is to narrow harder and decide exactly what problem you want to study.

## What is working well

### 1. The topic is clearly meaningful to you
That matters. You have a genuine interest in the subject, and you are not forcing a topic just because it sounds academic. Your familiarity with MMOs and with specific examples like **Final Fantasy XIV** gives the proposal potential access to concrete examples and real design concerns. That personal investment can become a strength if the topic is focused carefully.

### 2. There is a real RE problem here
The strongest underlying idea in the draft is that MMOs depend on more than satisfying traditional functional requirements. They also depend on producing and sustaining intended player experiences such as:
- engagement
- challenge
- progression
- retention
- reward balance

That creates a real requirements engineering problem, especially when systems can be “functionally correct” but still fail as player experiences.

### 3. The revision is better than the broadest possible version
You did narrow from “video games in general” toward **MMOs**, and you started to move toward the question of validating intended player experience. That is a good step. The proposal is stronger now than it would be if it were still trying to talk about all games, all genres, and all experience goals at once.

## What to strengthen

### 1. The proposal is still too broad
This is the biggest issue. Right now the draft is trying to do several things at once:
- discuss MMOs as a genre
- discuss player engagement and retention
- discuss economy/progression systems
- discuss failed launches
- discuss player experience validation
- discuss broad RE course concepts in game design

That is too much for this stage. The result is that the reader can see several interesting directions, but not one clear center. The next version needs to choose one.

### 2. The problem statement is still mixed with enthusiasm and background
Some of the material in the draft helps motivate the topic, but too much of it currently reads as context, fandom, or broad discussion rather than problem definition. For RP-1, the problem statement should be doing more of the following:
- identify a specific gap
- explain why that gap matters
- narrow the problem to one central question
- avoid drifting into too many examples that are not all serving the same argument

At the moment, the proposal still feels like it is discovering its real center as it goes.

### 3. The research questions need to be more explicit and focused
You gesture toward good questions, especially around:
- how developers validate intended player experience
- what evidence can be used to evaluate experience goals
- how this connects to requirements engineering

But the proposal needs those questions to be stated more sharply and more narrowly. Right now they are promising directions, but not yet as clean or disciplined as the strongest RP-1s in the set.

## Suggestions for the next iteration

### 1. Pick one center and commit to it
I think the strongest next move would be to choose one of these as the actual center:
- **How can developers validate intended player experience in MMOs?**
- **What evidence can be used to evaluate whether MMO systems produce intended engagement/retention outcomes?**
- **How can experience-related requirements in MMOs be specified and validated?**

Any one of those could work. Trying to do all three at once is what is making the proposal feel diffuse.

### 2. Reduce the number of examples
Right now the draft includes:
- Final Fantasy XIV
- Concord
- Anthem
- economy/progression discussion
- dailies, raids, PVP, missions
- failure statistics

That is a lot. The next version will be stronger if it uses fewer examples more strategically. You do not need to prove the whole game industry is risky. You just need enough context to motivate your specific research problem.

### 3. Make the research questions do more of the organizing work
In the next version, the proposal should be more clearly structured around 2–4 actual research questions that define the project. Once those are strong, the examples and background can be selected to support them rather than the other way around.

## Bottom line
This is a promising topic, but it is still too broad and exploratory for a strong final proposal. The next step is to narrow hard, reduce the number of competing examples and themes, and choose one precise line of inquiry about validating or specifying intended player experience in MMOs.
