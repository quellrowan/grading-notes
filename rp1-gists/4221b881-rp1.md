## Overall
This is a promising topic with a legitimate and interesting requirements engineering problem at its center. The proposal identifies an important tension in embedded and autonomous systems: safety and security requirements can both be essential, but they do not always align cleanly in practice. Focusing on autonomous UAV navigation in the DOD context gives the topic a serious and relevant application area.

At the same time, this draft still needs another round of tightening. The problem is interesting, but the proposal currently feels rougher and less focused than some of the stronger submissions. The biggest issues are clarity of writing, precision of framing, and making sure the questions, problem statement, and likely evidence all line up as cleanly as possible.

## What is working well

### 1. The central tension is real and meaningful
The idea that safety and security requirements may conflict in autonomous navigation is a strong problem. The proposal explains this tension clearly through examples such as:
- authentication/verification introducing latency
- fail-safe behavior potentially conflicting with security lockdown
- real-time responsiveness conflicting with protection against spoofing or manipulation

That is a strong requirements engineering angle because it naturally raises questions about tradeoffs, prioritization, and conflicting goals.

### 2. The operational context helps
Narrowing the topic to **autonomous navigation in DOD UAV systems** is a good move. It gives the project a concrete environment in which these tensions matter and helps avoid becoming too broad. This is much better than trying to talk about all UAV systems, all embedded systems, or all safety/security conflicts in general.

### 3. The response to feedback shows a useful narrowing move
The draft shows that you recognized the earlier proposal was too broad and responded by:
- making the safety/security conflict the center
- focusing on prioritization decisions
- anchoring the topic in autonomous navigation

That is the right direction and makes the project more manageable.

## What to strengthen

### 1. The writing needs to be cleaned up for clarity
This is the biggest weakness of the current draft. The underlying idea is strong, but the writing sometimes makes it harder than it should be to see the structure of the argument. There are several places where:
- wording is repetitive
- sentences are awkward or imprecise
- grammar issues make the reasoning less sharp than it actually is

That matters because in a proposal like this, clarity is part of the quality of the thinking. I can still see the idea, but the next version needs cleaner prose so the problem statement and questions feel more disciplined.

### 2. The proposal should be more explicit about the exact center of inquiry
You say the core question is how engineers prioritize safety and security requirements when they conflict. That is a good center. But parts of the draft still drift toward:
- cataloging failure types
- describing standards
- discussing examples
- identifying documented incidents

Those are all useful, but they should support the central question rather than compete with it. The next version should make it even clearer that the project is not trying to solve every safety-security issue in UAVs. It is trying to understand **how prioritization happens and where requirements processes fall short when conflicts emerge**.

### 3. The evidence base needs to become more realistic
Right now, the project gestures toward:
- case studies
- incident reports
- vulnerability analyses
- standards
- practitioner decision-making

That may be too much unless you define a narrower evidence strategy. For example, are you primarily studying:
- documented case examples?
- requirements and standards frameworks?
- tradeoff patterns described in literature?
- practitioner reasoning as reflected in published sources?

The final proposal will be stronger if it is more explicit about what kind of evidence is actually available and realistic for this assignment.

## Suggestions for the next iteration

### 1. Tighten the problem statement around prioritization
The strongest version of this proposal centers on one key issue:
- when safety and security requirements conflict, how are tradeoffs made, and what requirements practices help or fail in that process?

Everything else should support that. If a section does not help answer that question, it may be too peripheral.

### 2. Clarify the role of RQ2 and RQ3
Right now:
- **RQ1** is clearly the central question
- **RQ2** provides conflict categories and examples
- **RQ3** brings in real-world cases and consequences

That structure is reasonable, but in the next version you may want to tighten the wording so the supporting questions feel more clearly subordinate to RQ1. At the moment they are useful, but they could be integrated more cleanly.

### 3. Clean up terminology and prose before moving forward
This proposal would improve significantly just by becoming more polished at the sentence level. You already have a worthwhile research direction. The next version should make sure that the writing reflects that by:
- removing repetition
- sharpening phrasing
- tightening transitions
- and making the distinction between the problem, the examples, and the research questions more explicit

## Bottom line
This is a promising RP-1 with a strong central tension and a relevant context, but it still needs refinement. The next step is not to broaden the project further, but to sharpen the writing, tighten the focus around prioritization, and define a more realistic evidentiary path for the full proposal.
