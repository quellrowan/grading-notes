## What to strengthen

### 1. Be careful not to let the project become “all bypasses, all patterns, all systems”
Even though this draft is much better scoped than the original, the project could still widen again in the next stage if you are not careful. In particular, RQ3 starts pushing toward a broader claim about adversarial systems in general. That could be valuable, but it could also become too large for the final proposal if you try to fully generalize from one case.

For the next iteration, keep asking:
- What is my core case?
- What is my evidence base?
- What claims can I actually support from that evidence?

You do not need to solve “all adversarial requirements systems.” You need a strong, well-supported study of this one slice of the problem.

### 2. Clarify what counts as “foreseeable”
The word **foreseeable** is doing a lot of work here. It is a strong term, but in the full proposal you will need to be more explicit about how you will justify it. For example:
- documented prior bypass patterns?
- known stakeholder incentives?
- existing misuse-case methods?
- similar failures in earlier control systems?

Right now the argument is persuasive, but the next version should make the standard of foreseeability a little more explicit.

### 3. Tighten the line between evidence and conclusion
The draft is strongest when it says the bypasses are **evidence of possible requirements blind spots**. It is a little weaker when it starts to sound as though the conclusion is already fully proven. For the full proposal, keep the posture investigative:
- these bypasses suggest assumptions worth analyzing
- these cases may reveal requirements weaknesses
- this study asks whether systematic RE practices could have surfaced them

That tone will make the proposal sound more rigorous and less like a verdict delivered in advance.

## Suggestions for the next iteration

### 1. Define the likely evidence base
For the full proposal, start thinking concretely about what evidence you would examine. For example:
- documented bypass reports
- community discussions or repeated user reports
- prior parental-control system examples
- Apple feature interactions and design assumptions as reflected in product behavior
- RE concepts such as misuse cases, obstacle analysis, and stakeholder modeling

You do not need the full methodology yet, but you do need to start identifying what kind of evidence would support the claims.

### 2. Keep the bypass set small and intentional
Do not expand to every possible workaround. Choose a small number of bypasses that represent distinct types of requirements failure, such as:
- unrealistic behavioral assumptions
- cross-feature interactions
- administrative control assumptions
- client-side enforcement limitations

That will give the final paper stronger structure.

### 3. Make the likely contribution more explicit
As this moves forward, the contribution may be something like:
- showing how predictable misuse can be treated as a requirements problem rather than a post-release exploit problem
- identifying assumption patterns that weaken systems with conflicting stakeholders
- illustrating how specific RE practices might have surfaced these issues earlier

You do not need to fully finalize that yet, but it would help to begin stating what this project might contribute beyond the specific Apple case.

This is a strong RP-1. The topic is interesting, but more importantly, it is now **focused, researchable, and clearly grounded in requirements engineering**. The next step is not to widen it, but to keep the scope disciplined and make the evidentiary and analytical frame more explicit.
