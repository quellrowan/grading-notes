## Overall
This is a strong RP-1 with a clear and timely problem. The proposal does a good job identifying a real requirements engineering challenge in LLM-based assistants: these systems are useful and persuasive, but their behavior is variable enough that trust and oversight cannot be left implicit. The draft is especially effective when it frames the problem not simply as “LLMs make mistakes,” but as a requirements issue involving **human oversight, trust boundaries, and acceptable system use**.

This is a good direction for RP-1 because it shows clear narrowing from a broad AI topic into a more specific requirements problem. The next step will be to continue sharpening the boundaries of the project so it stays focused as it develops into a full proposal.

## What is working well

### 1. The problem statement is clear and well motivated
You identify a real problem and explain it in a way that makes sense from a requirements perspective. In particular, the proposal does a good job distinguishing between:
- the technical variability of LLM outputs
- and the requirements question of how systems should define trust, oversight, and boundaries of use

That distinction is important. It helps the project stay grounded in software requirements rather than becoming a general commentary on AI reliability.

### 2. The proposal is appropriately narrowed
The choice to focus on **human oversight and trust boundaries** gives the project a much clearer center than a broader draft about AI reliability, confidence, grounding, misuse, and system behavior all at once. This is a meaningful refinement. It also makes the problem more researchable, because you now have a clearer conceptual thread running through the statement and questions.

### 3. The research questions are generally well aligned
The questions fit the problem statement and stay in the same conceptual space:
- when human oversight is necessary
- what trust boundaries should be captured
- how these requirements differ from traditional software
- what misuse/over-reliance scenarios matter

That is a solid set of questions for this stage. They are conceptually coherent and clearly tied to the proposal’s focus.

### 4. The response to feedback is strong
You do a good job showing that you listened to the main concern—breadth—and responded by selecting one clearer center. The explanation of why you chose trust boundaries and oversight as the main focus is persuasive and makes the revision feel intentional rather than arbitrary.

## What to strengthen

### 1. The project still risks becoming broad if the concepts are not separated carefully
The main challenge for the next iteration is that several related ideas are still close together:
- trust
- oversight
- misuse
- over-reliance
- acceptable behavior
- system limits

These all belong in the same conversation, but they are not identical. If the project tries to handle all of them equally, it may spread out again. The next version should more clearly establish:
- what the primary concept is
- what supporting concepts are included only because they help define it

Right now, I read the main center as **oversight and trust boundaries**, but you should keep reinforcing that.

### 2. Some questions could become more precise
The questions are good, but a few still operate at a fairly high level. For example, “what kinds of trust boundaries” is conceptually interesting, but in the full proposal you will need to say more clearly what kinds of boundaries you have in mind. Are these:
- task boundaries?
- risk boundaries?
- decision boundaries?
- escalation/deference boundaries?
- communication/uncertainty boundaries?

You do not need to fully resolve that here, but the next version should begin to specify it.

### 3. Be careful not to drift into solution language too early
This draft is strongest when it is defining the problem. It is weaker if it starts to imply a broad design framework before the problem is fully bounded. For now, the proposal should keep its center on:
- identifying the requirements gap
- clarifying why current practices may be under-specifying oversight
- analyzing what kinds of boundaries matter

That is already a strong project. You do not need to solve the whole space yet.

## Suggestions for the next iteration

### 1. Clarify the type of LLM assistant you are most concerned with
Right now the examples are broad: customer support, technical guidance, internal decision support. That helps motivate the problem, but for the next version it may help to narrow the context slightly. For example:
- decision-support assistants
- technical/advisory assistants
- workplace-facing assistants
- software engineering assistants

You do not necessarily have to commit to one single product type, but some narrowing may improve focus.

### 2. Make “human oversight” more operational
The next version should start asking what human oversight actually means in this project. For example:
- review before action?
- confirmation before high-stakes output is used?
- escalation to human judgment under certain conditions?
- restrictions on autonomous use in certain tasks?

That will help the proposal move from a strong idea to a more analyzable one.

### 3. Think ahead about the evidence base
As you develop this further, consider what will ground the argument. For example:
- examples of LLM assistants in practice
- existing guidance or policy on human-in-the-loop use
- studies of over-trust or misuse
- comparisons to traditional software requirement practices

Again, you do not need full methods yet, but the project will benefit from a clearer sense of where the evidence will come from.

## Bottom line
This is a strong RP-1. The topic is timely, the RE angle is clear, and the narrowing is meaningful. The next step is to keep the project from broadening again by distinguishing more carefully between oversight, trust, misuse, and system boundaries, while making the central concept of human oversight more concrete.
